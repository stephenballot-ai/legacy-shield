## The Digital Frontier of Estate Planning

In 2026, the question is no longer *if* you have digital assets, but *how* they will be passed on. We live our lives in the cloud, but European law has often struggled to keep pace with our digital footprints. As we cross into the mid-2020s, the landscape of digital inheritance in Europe is finally reaching a turning point.

Whether you are an expat living in Amsterdam, a digital nomad working from Lisbon, or a privacy-conscious citizen in Berlin, understanding the current legal reality of your digital estate is critical. The "grey zones" are shrinking, but the responsibility to act still rests firmly on your shoulders.

## The EU Succession Regulation: A Digital Upgrade?

The foundation of cross-border estate planning in Europe remains the **EU Succession Regulation (No 650/2012)**. While originally designed for physical property and bank accounts, courts across the continent are increasingly applying its principles to digital data.

The core principle remains: the law of the country where you had your "habitual residence" at the time of death applies to your entire estate. For the millions of expats in Europe, this means your digital legacy is likely governed by the laws of your adopted home, not necessarily your country of citizenship.

## Country Spotlight: The Fragmented Reality

Despite EU-wide regulations, specific national laws and court rulings vary significantly:

### Germany: The Trailblazer
Germany remains the gold standard for digital inheritance rights following the landmark 2018 Federal Court of Justice (BGH) ruling. The court decided that digital accounts—including social media—are part of the inheritance just like paper letters or diaries. In 2026, German heirs have the strongest legal standing to demand access from tech giants, provided they have a certificate of inheritance (*Erbschein*).

### France: The Right to Digital Death
France was ahead of its time with the **Loi pour une République Numérique** in 2016. It grants individuals the right to give "digital death" instructions. In 2026, French law remains unique in its focus on the "post-mortem right to privacy," allowing you to strictly forbid your heirs from accessing certain data, or conversely, empowering them with specific mandates.

### The Netherlands: A Practical Shift
In the Netherlands, there is still no specific "Digital Inheritance Act," but the trend has shifted toward practical administration. Dutch courts increasingly treat digital access as a matter of "good administration" of the estate. However, without explicit mention in a will or a digital codicil, families often face uphill battles with US-based platforms.

### Spain: The Law of Digital Wills
Spain's **LOPDGDD (2018)** explicitly recognizes the right of heirs to access the digital contents of a deceased person. By 2026, the use of "Digital Wills" in Spain has become a standard recommendation by notaries, especially in regions like Catalonia which have even more specific local succession rules.

## The Great 2026 Challenge: Service Providers vs. Heirs

Even with favorable local laws, the biggest hurdle remains the **Terms of Service (ToS)** of major platforms like Apple, Google, and Meta. 

Most tech giants are headquartered in the United States and operate under "contracts" that frequently claim accounts are non-transferable. While the EU is exerting more pressure through the **Digital Services Act (DSA)** to force these companies to respect European inheritance laws, the reality on the ground is often a months-long struggle of death certificates, court orders, and "unresponsive" support bots.
