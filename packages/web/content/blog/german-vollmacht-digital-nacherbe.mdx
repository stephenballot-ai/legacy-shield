## The Document You Think Protects Everything

You signed a *Vollmacht* — a German power of attorney. Maybe it was a *Vorsorgevollmacht*, the kind that covers healthcare decisions and financial management if you become incapacitated. Maybe a broader *Generalvollmacht* giving someone authority over your affairs.

Either way, you probably think it covers everything.

It almost certainly doesn't cover your digital life.

## What a Vollmacht Is — and What It Isn't

A *Vollmacht* (power of attorney) in Germany allows a designated person — your *Bevollmächtigter* — to act on your behalf in legal and financial matters. Unlike the French *procuration* or English power of attorney, the German *Vollmacht* can be drafted privately (a *Privatvollmacht*) or notarially certified (a *notarielle Vollmacht*), depending on the scope of authority required.

The *Vorsorgevollmacht* — a precautionary power of attorney — is particularly important because without it, a German court will appoint a legal guardian (*Betreuer*) if you become incapacitated, even if you have a trusted spouse or partner. Germany's legal system does not automatically assume your spouse can act for you. If there's no *Vollmacht*, there's a court process. Every time.

But here is what most *Vollmachten* — even carefully drafted ones — do not address:

**Your cryptocurrency wallets.** A *Vollmacht* gives your *Bevollmächtigter* legal authority. But legal authority means nothing to a blockchain. Access to your Bitcoin requires your private key or seed phrase. Your designated representative can show up at a notary's office with a perfectly valid *Vollmacht* and still be completely unable to access your crypto. The authorization and the access are two entirely different things.

**Your online accounts.** Google, Apple, and most major platforms are American companies, governed by US Terms of Service. A German *Vollmacht* carries no legal weight with these companies. Facebook does not have a department that processes German legal documents. Apple's support team cannot verify a *Vollmacht*. Even a notarially certified document may simply be ignored.

**Your digital business.** If you run a business online — a Shopify store, a SaaS product, a content platform — a *Vollmacht* might give your representative authority in German law, but zero access to the platforms themselves. Without your login credentials, the business grinds to a halt.

## The Nachlass Problem: When You Die

The *Vollmacht* only applies during your lifetime. After death, it becomes irrelevant — at that point, the rules of the *Erbrecht* (inheritance law) under the *BGB* (Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch) take over.

Under German *Erbrecht*, heirs inherit all assets including digital ones. But "inheriting" a digital asset and actually *accessing* that digital asset are different problems.

The 2018 *Bundesgerichtshof* ruling (BGH III ZR 183/17) established that digital accounts can be inherited — a landmark decision that clarified the legal principle. But legal inheritance does not equal practical access. A court ruling that confirms you own your father's Facebook account does not give you his password.

This is the gap that neither a *Vollmacht* nor a *Testament* fully solves, because both documents deal in legal authority — not in the cryptographic keys or passwords that actually control digital assets.

## The Specific Risk for Expats in Germany

If you are an expat living in Germany, your digital life is likely even more fragmented than that of a German national. You may have:

- A pension or investment account in your home country
- A PayPal account registered to an old address
- A crypto wallet created before you moved to Germany
- Files stored on American servers under a different country's privacy laws
- A German bank account that your *Vollmacht* covers, alongside five others that it doesn't

Your German *Bevollmächtigter* has authority within the German legal system. That authority does not follow you onto the blockchain or into the Terms of Service of American platforms.

## What Needs to Happen — Practically

The legal document and the access document need to be separate. Your *Vollmacht* handles the legal authority. You need a complementary system that handles the access.

**Create a digital asset inventory.** List everything with economic or sentimental value: crypto wallets, online investment accounts, email accounts, social media, cloud storage, subscriptions, domain names, digital business assets. Note the rough value and how to access each one.

**Store credentials securely and separately.** Your seed phrases and passwords should never appear in a *Vollmacht* or *Testament*. These become public or semi-public documents. Instead, use an encrypted vault designed for secure handoff.

**Name a digital representative.** This can be your *Bevollmächtigter* or a different person entirely — ideally someone with genuine technical competence. Give them specific instructions, not just legal authority.

**Make the two documents speak to each other.** Your *Vollmacht* or *Testament* can reference a separate digital estate plan: "The principal has established a digital asset plan held in an encrypted vault, accessible to the designated digital representative." This creates a legal bridge without exposing your credentials in a public document.
