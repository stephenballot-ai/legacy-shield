## The Digital Ghost in the Machine

You’ve spent decades building a digital life. Thousands of photos on Google, a lifetime of conversations on WhatsApp, and a carefully curated library on Kindle. Under the **General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)**, you have a powerful "Right to Data Portability." You can demand any company hand over your data in a structured, machine-readable format.

But here is the catch that most people—and even many lawyers—miss: **GDPR rights are for living persons only.**

The moment you pass away, your legal "personality" under EU law ceases to exist. Your heirs don't automatically inherit your GDPR rights. This creates a terrifying "data portability gap" where your most precious digital memories can be locked away forever, even if your family has a legal will.

## Why Your Family Can’t Just "Log In"

Many people assume their family can simply use their passwords to download their data. While this happens in practice, it often violates the **Terms of Service** of major tech platforms. 

If Google or Apple detects an "unauthorized" login after a death, they may lock the account permanently for security reasons. Without a clear legal path to data portability, your heirs are at the mercy of Silicon Valley's internal policies—which are often far more restrictive than European law.

## The European Legal Grey Zone

Across the EU, the rules for "digital inheritance" are a patchwork:
- **In Germany**, the Federal Court of Justice ruled in 2018 that digital accounts are part of the estate and pass to heirs (the "Facebook case").
- **In France**, the Digital Republic Act allows individuals to give instructions for the "erasure, storage, and communication" of their data after death.
- **In most other EU countries**, the law is silent.

This means if you are an expat living in the Netherlands, Spain, or Italy, your family might face a brick wall when trying to recover your data. They have the right to your physical photo albums, but they may have zero legal right to the 50,000 photos stored in your cloud account.

## How to Bridge the Gap Today

You cannot rely on the law to catch up with technology. You need to take proactive steps to ensure your data portability rights don't die with you.

### 1. Use "Legacy Contacts" Where They Exist
Platforms like Apple and Google have built-in "Legacy Contact" or "Inactive Account Manager" tools. Use them. These are essentially private contracts that bypass the GDPR gap by giving specific people permission to download your data before the account is deleted.

### 2. Explicitly Mention "Digital Assets" in Your Will
Don't just leave "all my property" to your spouse. Your will should explicitly mention "digital assets, including but not limited to cloud storage, social media accounts, and personal data." While this isn't a silver bullet in every jurisdiction, it gives your heirs a much stronger legal standing when fighting a tech giant.

### 3. Create a Digital Inventory
Your family can't request data they don't know exists. Keep a secure record of every platform where you store meaningful data. This isn't just about passwords—it's about knowing which "digital doors" need to be opened.
